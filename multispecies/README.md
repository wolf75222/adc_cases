# multispecies: two heterogeneous fluids coupled by a single system Poisson

A periodic `adc.System` advances two species described by different physical models at the same
time (electrons in 4-variable compressible Euler, ions in 3-variable isothermal Euler), coupled
by a single elliptic problem whose right-hand side aggregates the charges of both species. The
invariant under test is per-species mass conservation: even while sharing the same electric
field, each fluid conserves its own mass independently, to floating-point precision.

## 1. Why this invariant: per-species mass conserved under coupling

Justifies the Proves clause (the mass of each species is conserved to $10^{-9}$).

The case exists to answer a structural question: when two species share the same electric field
$\mathbf{E}=-\nabla\phi$, do their mass balances stay decoupled? The answer is yes, and the
reason is that the field enters only the momentum source, never the continuity equation. Each
species $s$ obeys

$$\partial_t n_s+\nabla\cdot(n_s\mathbf{v}_s)=0,\qquad \partial_t(n_s\mathbf{v}_s)+\nabla\cdot(\dots)=\frac{q_s}{m}n_s\mathbf{E}.$$

The force $\frac{q_s}{m}n_s\mathbf{E}$ appears on the right-hand side of momentum, not of
continuity: it redistributes momentum (and, for the 4-variable electrons, the work
$\frac{q_s}{m}n_s\mathbf{v}_s\cdot\mathbf{E}$ in the energy), but the right-hand side of
$\partial_t n_s+\nabla\cdot(n_s\mathbf{v}_s)=0$ stays exactly zero. The mass of each species is
therefore conserved independently, and the shared $\phi$ coupling cannot transfer mass from one
species to the other. The discrete derivation of this conservation (section 4) shows why the
finite-volume scheme inherits exactly this property, up to rounding.

This is not a reproduced instability: the dynamics over 20 steps are tiny (see section 6). The
only falsifiable object is the invariant. The initial charge separation (modulated electrons,
uniform ions) only serves to make the coupled Poisson non-trivial: if $f\equiv 0$, the field
would be zero and the demo would test nothing, hence the `assert qmax0 > 1e-6` (in run.py).

## 2. The equations and who computes them

Justifies the Inputs clause (the two species models + the system Poisson).

| Block | Equation solved | Brick (`models.py`) |
|---|---|---|
| electrons (4 var $\rho,\rho u,\rho v,E$) | $\partial_t\rho+\nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{v})=0$; $\partial_t(\rho\mathbf{v})+\nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}+p\mathbf{I})=q_e\rho\mathbf{E}$; $\partial_t E+\nabla\cdot((E+p)\mathbf{v})=q_e\rho\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{E}$; $p=(\gamma-1)(E-\tfrac12\rho\lvert\mathbf{v}\rvert^2)$ | `electron_euler(charge=-1, gamma=5/3)` |
| ions (3 var $\rho,\rho u,\rho v$) | $\partial_t\rho+\nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{v})=0$; $\partial_t(\rho\mathbf{v})+\nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}+c_s^2\rho\mathbf{I})=q_i\rho\mathbf{E}$ (no energy equation: closure via $c_s^2$) | `ion_isothermal(charge=+1, cs2=1.0)` |
| elliptic (system) | $\nabla^2\phi=f=q_e n_e+q_i n_i$, $\mathbf{E}=-\nabla\phi$; periodic | `set_poisson(rhs="charge_density")` |

A species model is a composition of four generic bricks via
`adc.Model(state, transport, source, elliptic)` (`Model` in the `adc` package, provided by adc_cpp):

| Model | `state` | `transport` | `source` | `elliptic` |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `electron_euler` (in [`models.py`](../adc_cases/models.py)) | `FluidState(kind="compressible", gamma=5/3)` | `CompressibleFlux()` | `PotentialForce(charge=-1)` | `ChargeDensity(charge=-1)` |
| `ion_isothermal` (in [`models.py`](../adc_cases/models.py)) | `FluidState(kind="isothermal", cs2=1.0)` | `IsothermalFlux()` | `PotentialForce(charge=+1)` | `ChargeDensity(charge=+1)` |

The same $q$ drives both `PotentialForce(charge=q)` (force $\frac{q}{m}\rho\mathbf{E}$,
in __init__.py) and
`ChargeDensity(charge=q)` (contribution $q\,n$ to the Poisson right-hand side,
in __init__.py): this is what makes the
coupling physically consistent. Who computes what:

| `run.py` symbol | Layer | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| `add_block("electrons", model=models.electron_euler(...), spatial=Spatial(minmod), time=Explicit())` (in run.py); same for ions | Python composes | choice of the model per block, of the scheme (MUSCL minmod + Rusanov), of the integrator (SSPRK2); the two blocks are heterogeneous (4 var vs 3 var) |
| `models.electron_euler` / `ion_isothermal` -> `CompressibleFlux` / `IsothermalFlux` / `PotentialForce` / `ChargeDensity` (`models.py`, bricks from module `adc`) | C++ brick fixes the physics | exact convention for the flux, the closure ($\gamma$ or $c_s^2$), the force $\frac{q}{m}\rho\mathbf{E}$, the $q\,n$ term injected into the Poisson |
| `assemble_rhs<minmod, rusanov>` + system Poisson `geometric_mg` ($f=\sum_b q_b n_b$) | per-cell kernel (device) | the real computation: residual $-\nabla\cdot F+S$ per cell and multigrid solve, with no Python callback in the hot path |

`set_poisson(rhs="charge_density", solver="geometric_mg")` (in run.py) configures a system
Poisson whose right-hand side is the sum of each block's elliptic bricks. The token
`"charge_density"` is the bit-identical alias of the generic composite right-hand side
($\texttt{ChargeDensitySource}$ inherits from $\texttt{CompositeRhs}$,
in __init__.py) when all blocks
carry a charge density: both take the same numerical path on the C++ side.

## 3. The code, function by function

Everything lives in `main()` of [`run.py`](run.py). The import plumbing (the `try/except`
`import adc_cases`, `sys.path` fallback) is not glossed. The load-bearing lines:

System composition (in run.py):

```python
sim = adc.System(n=48, L=1.0, periodic=True)                       # maillage seul
sim.add_block("electrons", model=models.electron_euler(charge=-1.0, gamma=5.0/3.0),
              spatial=adc.Spatial(minmod=True), time=adc.Explicit())
sim.add_block("ions", model=models.ion_isothermal(charge=+1.0, cs2=1.0),
              spatial=adc.Spatial(minmod=True), time=adc.Explicit())
sim.set_poisson(rhs="charge_density", solver="geometric_mg")       # un Poisson de systeme
```
- `adc.System(n, L, periodic)` carries only the mesh ($48^2$, the square $[0,1]^2$, periodic).
  The physical parametrization ($\gamma$, $c_s^2$, charge) lives in the bricks, not in the system
  config.
- the two `add_block` calls produce two heterogeneous compiled advance closures: 4 conservative
  variables for the electrons, 3 for the ions. The system advances them side by side without
  homogenizing them.
- `set_poisson(rhs="charge_density")` wires the right-hand side $f=\sum_b q_b n_b$: this is the
  only point where the two species meet.

Initial condition, charge separation (in run.py):

```python
n = sim.nx()
x = (np.arange(n) + 0.5) / n * 1.0           # centres de cellules le long de axis=1
ne = 1.0 + 0.02 * np.cos(2.0 * np.pi * x / 1.0)   # electrons modules, delta = 0.02
ne2d = np.broadcast_to(ne, (n, n)).copy()    # x varie le long des colonnes
ni2d = np.ones((n, n))                       # ions uniformes
sim.set_density("electrons", ne2d); sim.set_density("ions", ni2d)
```
- `set_density(name, array)` sets only the density; the fluid stays at rest (zero momentum,
  energy at the model's closure value). This is why the later dynamics stay small in amplitude.
- the electron perturbation $\cos(2\pi x)$ with uniform ions creates
  $f=q_e n_e+q_i n_i=(-1)(1+\delta\cos)+(+1)(1)=-\delta\cos(2\pi x)$, hence a non-zero right-hand
  side that drives the coupled Poisson.

Diagnostics and verification (in run.py):

```python
mass_e0 = sim.mass("electrons"); mass_i0 = sim.mass("ions")        # masses initiales
...
sim.advance(dt, nsteps)                                            # 20 pas, boucle compilee C++
...
drift_e = assert_mass_conserved(mass_e1, mass_e0, tol=1e-9, label="electrons", relative=False)

```
- `sim.mass(name)` is the cell sum $\sum_{ij}\rho_{ij}$ (not the integral $\sum\rho\,dx\,dy$):
  verified, `mass("electrons") == np.sum(density("electrons")) == 2304.0` exactly
  (= $48\times48$, the cosine integrating to 0 over the period). This is the scale the absolute

- `assert_mass_conserved(..., relative=False)`
  (in [`checks.py`](../adc_cases/common/checks.py)) compares $\lvert M-M_0\rvert$ against `tol=1e-9` and
  returns the drift; `relative=False` keeps the historical absolute behavior.
- `assert_finite` / `assert_positive` (in [`checks.py`](../adc_cases/common/checks.py)): no
  NaN/Inf, densities strictly $>0$.

## 4. Math: why each mass is conserved up to rounding

Justifies the Proves clause and the $10^{-9}$ tolerance (Guaranteed invariants clause).

### 4.1 Flux telescoping on the torus

The finite-volume scheme updates the density of each cell $(i,j)$ by a flux balance across its
four faces:

$$\rho_{ij}^{n+1}=\rho_{ij}^{n}-\frac{dt}{h}\Big(F^x_{i+\frac12,j}-F^x_{i-\frac12,j}+F^y_{i,j+\frac12}-F^y_{i,j-\frac12}\Big),$$

where $F^x_{i+\frac12,j}$ is the numerical flux (Rusanov, MUSCL minmod reconstruction) crossing
the face between $(i,j)$ and $(i{+}1,j)$. The total mass of a species is the cell sum
$M=\sum_{ij}\rho_{ij}$ (exactly what `sim.mass` returns). Summing the update over all cells, each
interior flux appears twice with opposite signs: $F^x_{i+\frac12,j}$ leaves $(i,j)$ and enters
$(i{+}1,j)$. The sum telescopes:

$$M^{n+1}-M^{n}=-\frac{dt}{h}\sum_{ij}\big(\text{outgoing fluxes}-\text{incoming fluxes}\big)=-\frac{dt}{h}\big(\text{flux on the domain boundary}\big).$$

On a periodic domain the domain boundary does not exist: the right face of the last column is
identified with the left face of the first, and the boundary term cancels exactly by construction
(the flux leaving on the right enters on the left, same value, opposite signs). So
$M^{n+1}=M^{n}$ in exact arithmetic. This is a per-cell conservation property (a single face flux
shared between two neighboring cells), independent of the limiter, the Riemann flux and the time
integrator.

### 4.2 What the coupling changes, and what it does not

The source $S_s=\frac{q_s}{m}n_s\mathbf{E}$ (and the work for the energy) modifies only the
momentum (and energy) components of the state vector, not the density component:
`PotentialForce` acts on $\rho\mathbf{v}$ (and $E$), never on $\rho$. The telescoping of 4.1
applies to the continuity equation alone, whose right-hand side is zero. The shared field $\phi$
therefore cannot transfer mass: each species conserves its own, and the two balances are
decoupled. This is exactly the invariant under test, and it is why it holds even with a shared
Poisson.

The residual drift is not zero, because of floating-point arithmetic: the telescoping sum of
$\sim 2300$ terms does not cancel down to the last bit (floating-point addition is not
associative, the reduction order introduces a residual). The $10^{-9}$ tolerance sits between:
- the floating-point grain at this scale: $1\,\text{ulp}(M_0)=\varepsilon_{\text{mach}}\cdot 2304\approx5.1\times10^{-13}$;
- the magnitude a real mass leak would have (of the order of the dynamics, $\sim10^{-2}$).

The measured drift ($\max_t\lvert\Delta M_e\rvert=4.23\times10^{-11}\approx 80\,\text{ulp}$,
$\max_t\lvert\Delta M_i\rvert=3.18\times10^{-11}\approx 62\,\text{ulp}$) lives in this window:
20 to 80 times the machine grain (accumulation over 20 steps and one MG solve per step), and 23
times below the $10^{-9}$ tolerance. In relative terms, $4.23\times10^{-11}/2304\approx1.8\times10^{-14}$, i.e. $\sim80\,\varepsilon_{\text{mach}}$:
this is rounding noise, not a physical leak. A drift exceeding $10^{-9}$ would betray a
conservation bug (an unshared face flux, a badly re-stitched periodic boundary term, a source
writing into $\rho$).

### 4.3 The coupled Poisson, verified analytically

The right-hand side is $f=-\delta\cos(kx)$ with $k=2\pi/L$. On the torus, $\nabla^2\phi=f$ solves
mode by mode: $-k^2\hat\phi=-\delta$, hence $\phi(x)=(\delta/k^2)\cos(kx)$, with continuous peak
$\delta/k^2=0.02/(2\pi)^2=5.066\times10^{-4}$ (sampled at the cell centers, the discrete max is
$5.055\times10^{-4}$, the extremum falling between two cells). The sign convention is
$\varepsilon\nabla^2\phi=f$ with $\varepsilon=1$ (`DivEpsGrad` in __init__.py), not
$-\nabla^2\phi=f$: verified by the behavior (section 6, figure 3), where the measured $\phi(x)$
cut follows $+(\delta/k^2)\cos(kx)$, not its opposite. The measured potential has amplitude
$\lvert\phi\rvert_{\max}=5.025\times10^{-4}$ against the discrete analytic $5.055\times10^{-4}$:
a gap of $3.0\times10^{-6}$ ($\sim0.6\%$), due to the multigrid discretization on $48^2$ (this is
not a bit equality, only a consistency of sign and order of magnitude).

> Solvability: on a periodic grid, $\nabla^2\phi=f$ admits a solution only if
> $\int f=0$. Here $f=-\delta\cos(kx)$ integrates to 0 over the period (zero mean net charge,
> since $n_e$ and $n_i$ have the same mean 1): the periodic Poisson is well posed.

## 5. Numerical method

Justifies the Inputs clause (scheme) and the robustness of the invariant.

The numerical choice is made per block, independently of the model. Here the two blocks share the
same scheme, but the API would allow differing them (cf. the `two_fluid` recipe in
[`recipes.py`](../adc_cases/recipes.py): VanLeer+HLLC on the electrons, minmod on the ions).

- Reconstruction: MUSCL limited by minmod (`adc.Spatial(minmod=True)`) on the conservative
  variables, for both blocks.
- Riemann flux: Rusanov (default of `adc.Spatial`), robust across any transport.
- Temporal: `adc.Explicit()` = SSPRK2 (Shu-Osher, 2 stages, order 2), `substeps=1`,
  `stride=1`. No multirate.
- Poisson: $\nabla^2$ operator ($\varepsilon=1$), `charge_density` right-hand side
  ($\sum_b q_b n_b$), geometric multigrid solver `geometric_mg`. The `fft` alternative
  (periodic, $n=2^k$) is inapplicable here ($n=48$ is not a power of 2).
- Advance: `sim.advance(dt=0.001, nsteps=20)`, time loop compiled in C++ (no Python integrator
  here, unlike `composition` or `custom_scheme`).

None of these bricks breaks the telescoping of 4.1: minmod, Rusanov and SSPRK2 all operate on
shared face fluxes, so per-cell conservation is preserved up to rounding, whatever the choice.

## 6. Figures (regenerated by `make_figures.py`, in `figures/`)

`make_figures.py` replays the physics of `run.py` (same bricks, same IC, same 20 steps) while
recording per-species mass **at every step**, then plots three diagnostics. Exact command in
section 7. The numbers cited come from `figures/provenance.json`.

### `masse.png`: per-species conservation, absolute scale

![Mass drift M(t)-M(0) per species, axis in 1e-10, oscillating around zero](figures/masse.png)

It plots $M_s(t)-M_s(0)$ (the offset $M_0=2304$ is in the title), scaled in $10^{-10}$ to make
visible a drift that would be invisible at the scale of $2304$.

- **Proves**: the two curves oscillate at the level of $\sim4\times10^{-11}$ (electrons) and
  $\sim3\times10^{-11}$ (ions) around zero, i.e. a rounding walk of a few tens of ulps, 23 times
  below the $10^{-9}$ tolerance. This is the numerical proof that each species conserves its mass
  independently despite the shared Poisson (the `assert_mass_conserved` calls in run.py).
- **Suggested** (not asserted): the random-walk shape (no monotone drift) is consistent with
  rounding accumulation rather than a systematic leak; no `assert` tests for the absence of a
  trend, only the maximum.
- **Not shown**: the mass beyond 20 steps (the case stops at $t=0.02$); nothing guarantees the
  drift would stay $<10^{-9}$ over $10^5$ steps (rounding accumulation grows slowly).

### `densite.png`: final $n_e$ and $n_i$ maps

![Final density maps: n_e modulated in x with amplitude 0.02, n_i amplitude 4e-6 in opposition](figures/densite.png)

- **Proves**: $n_e\in[0.980,1.020]$ (the initial cosine of amplitude $0.02$, invariant in $y$) and
  $n_i\in[1{-}4\times10^{-6},\,1{+}4\times10^{-6}]$ stay finite and strictly positive
  (`assert_finite` + `assert_positive` in run.py).
- **Suggested**: $n_i$, initially uniform, has developed a modulation $\sim4\times10^{-6}$ (a
  million times smaller than the electrons') in phase opposition, a sign that the ions respond to
  the field $\phi$ created by the electrons. This is the visual signature of the inter-species
  coupling, but no `assert` measures this amplitude or its phase.
- **Not shown**: momentum and energy (not plotted); the nonlinear dynamics (20 steps, tiny
  amplitude); any comparison to a reference solution.

### `potentiel.png`: a single Poisson aggregates both charges

![|phi| map with two nodes, and phi(x) cut sticking to (delta/k^2)cos(kx)](figures/potentiel.png)

- **Proves**: the $\phi(x)$ cut (points) follows the analytic solution $(\delta/k^2)\cos(kx)$
  (line) with a residual $3.0\times10^{-6}$ ($\sim0.6\%$), and the $\lvert\phi\rvert$ map shows the
  two nodes ($\phi=0$ at $x=0.25$ and $x=0.75$) of the $\cos(2\pi x)$ mode. This confirms the
  sign convention $\nabla^2\phi=f$ (section 4.3) and that the system Poisson does solve the
  aggregated right-hand side.
- **Suggested**: the measured amplitude $5.025\times10^{-4}$, close to the analytic
  $5.055\times10^{-4}$, suggests the multigrid has converged; the $0.6\%$ residual is $48^2$
  truncation error, not a bit equality.
- **Not shown**: no `assert` in `run.py` tests $\phi$ (the potential is verified only by this
  figure, outside CI); no comparison to a reference FFT solver; no convergence study in $h$.
